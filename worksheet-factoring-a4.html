    • Practice using divisibility rules.
    • Identify factors of numbers.

    4. Word Problem.

    Sean wants to build a rectangular patio using big concrete square tiles. If he has 12 tiles, how many different rectangles can he make without cutting a tile or having any left over? It may be helpful to draw pictures or create a model.

    5. Factorizations.

    A factorization of a number \(n\) is a multiplication statement showing numbers that multiply to get \(n\text{.}\) For example, a factorization of \(6\) is \(2 \cdot 3\text{.}\) In the previous problem, you used geometry to find factorizations of 12. For each of the following numbers, find two different factorizations.
    (a)
    8
    (b)
    14
    (c)
    45

    6. Factor/Sum Puzzle.

    In each row, find two factors whose product is the number in the first column and whose sum is the number in the last column. The first row is done for you as an example.
    Product Factor Factor Sum
    \(36\) \(12\) \(3\) \(15\)
    \(6\) \(5\)
    \(12\) \(7\)
    \(24\) \(11\)
    \(15\) \(16\)
    \(81\) \(30\)
    \(-18\) \(7\)
    \(36\) \(-12\)
    The last two are tricky! Remember that you can ask your instructor or classmates for help.

    7. Self Reflection.

    On a scale of 1-5, how confident are you with the material in this worksheet?
    Were any of the exercises confusing or frustrating? If so, identify them here and write down your plan to master those topics.
